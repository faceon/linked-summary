Summaries you can verify — each linked back to its source.

Product · Chrome Built-in AI · Local first

AI summarizers made it incredibly easy to skim long articles, but something got lost on the way: readers can’t always tell where a specific sentence actually came from. That single gap is what makes a “convenient” summary different from a “trustworthy” one.

For most people, the ideal experience is simple: read the short version first, and jump to the original paragraph when something looks interesting or suspicious. That’s exactly what Linked Summary tries to recreate — inside the browser, without sending the page to a server.

Why local?

Chrome’s built-in AI lets us process the current tab directly on the device, which means the page you’re reading never leaves your computer. This matters for internal docs, paywalled pages, or anything you don’t want to upload.

Linking summaries back to source

When Linked Summary generates key points, it also stores a pointer to the DOM segment that most likely produced that sentence. So when a user clicks on the key point in the side panel, the original text is revealed and softly highlighted.

This keeps the AI helpful, but also makes it accountable — you can see what it saw.

How users see it

The flow is intentionally short: open a page → click the extension → read the linked summary. No extra windows, no copy-pasting, just side-panel reading. That’s important for people who read several articles in a row.

This page is a demo. In the real extension the right panel is injected next to the page you’re reading.
